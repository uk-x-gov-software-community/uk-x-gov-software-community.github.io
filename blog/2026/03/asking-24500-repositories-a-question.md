We're delighted to highlight some fantastic work by community member Chris Nesbitt-Smith, who has written about how he built the [xgov Open Source Repo Scraper](https://www.uk-x-gov-software-community.org.uk/xgov-opensource-repo-scraper/) — a tool that asks a single question of over 24,500 government repositories and surfaces the answers.
 
The tool, which is [hosted on our community GitHub](https://github.com/uk-x-gov-software-community/xgov-opensource-repo-scraper), crawls open source repositories published by UK government organisations and aggregates information about them in one place. Chris's article on LinkedIn explores what happens when you put that data to work: what patterns emerge, what questions you can answer, and what insights you gain when you look across the breadth of government software engineering at scale.

### Why this matters

It can be hard to get a clear picture of what's happening across government software engineering. Thousands of repositories exist across dozens of departments and agencies, each going about their work largely independently. Tools like this one change that. By collecting and querying repository metadata at scale, the scraper makes it possible to:

- Spot trends in the languages, frameworks, and tooling government teams are using
- Identify where similar problems exist especially now he has added sbom support 
- Find opportunities for reuse and collaboration that would otherwise be invisible
- Understand how government's open source footprint is growing over time
As i write this pist I realised some of my own department licensing practices need some work, purely because this tool showed me instantly where licenses were missing.
This is exactly the kind of work our community exists to support: practical, open, and useful to everyone working in government software engineering.

### Get involved

The tool lives at [uk-x-gov-software-community/xgov-opensource-repo-scraper](https://github.com/uk-x-gov-software-community/xgov-opensource-repo-scraper) and contributions are welcome. Whether you want to add new queries, improve the data collection, or just explore what's already there — the data is open and the code is yours to use.

You can read Chris's full write-up on [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/asking-24500-repositories-question-chris-nesbitt-smith-fo4we). A huge thank you to Chris for building this and for sharing his thinking so openly with the community.
